# Should You Leave Australia? A Data-Driven Analysis of the Aging Population Crisis

*A 35-year-old parent's guide to navigating Australia's demographic and economic challenges*

---

If you're a millennial parent living in Sydney, you've probably had this thought at 2am while scrolling through property listings: "Can we actually afford to stay here?"

You're not paranoid. The numbers tell a stark story.

I recently analyzed the 2021 Australian Census data to answer a question many young families are asking: Given Australia's aging population and sky-high housing costs, should we stay or go?

What I found surprised me. The situation is serious—but not hopeless. Here's what the data reveals, and what it means for your future.

## The Aging Crisis By The Numbers

Let's start with the uncomfortable truth: Australia is getting old, fast.

**Current State (2021 Census):**
- Total population: 25.4 million
- Median age: 38 years
- People aged 65+: 4.4 million (17.2%)
- Prime working age (35-54): 6.7 million (26.5%)

That last number is crucial. If you're between 35-54, you're part of Australia's largest working-age cohort. You're also the generation that will bear the brunt of supporting an aging population.

The dependency ratio tells the real story: **For every 100 working-age Australians, there are 26.7 retirees to support.**

By 2040, experts project this will rise to 40-45 retirees per 100 workers. That's a 50-70% increase in just two decades.

### What Does This Mean For Your Wallet?

More retirees mean:
- Higher taxes to fund aged pensions
- Increased healthcare costs (aged care is expensive)
- Pressure on government services
- Potential reduction in quality of public infrastructure and services

If you're 35 now, you'll be 50-55 when this hits hardest—right when you should be in your peak earning and wealth-building years.

## The Sydney Housing Trap

Now layer on Australia's housing affordability crisis.

**National medians (2021):**
- Monthly mortgage: $1,863
- Weekly rent: $375
- Family income: $2,120/week ($110,240/year)

Sydney's numbers are significantly worse. The city regularly ranks in the top 10 globally for housing unaffordability, with a median price-to-income ratio of 10-13x.

Translation: If you earn the median family income of $110k, a median Sydney house costs $1.1-1.4 million. That's 10-13 years of your gross income.

For context, anything above 5x is considered "severely unaffordable."

### Can You Out-Earn The Problem?

Here's where it gets interesting. I analyzed income data specifically for the 35-44 age group—the prime earning years.

**Income distribution for ages 35-44:**
- 22.7% earn $104k+ per year ($2,000+/week)
- 5.5% earn $182k+ per year ($3,500+/week)
- 27.8% earn $52k-91k (middle class range)

If you can break into the top 25% of earners for your age group, housing becomes manageable. A $150k individual income (or $200k+ household with a partner) can absorb Sydney's housing costs.

But that's a big "if." It requires either:
- High-value employment (tech, finance, senior management, specialized skills)
- Successful entrepreneurship
- Or both (employment plus side business)

## The Employment vs. Entrepreneurship Question

One of the most common questions I hear: "Should I get a stable job or start a business?"

The data suggests a hybrid approach works best.

**The Three-Phase Strategy:**

**Phase 1 (Ages 35-37): Employment Focus**
- Get highest-paying job possible ($100k-$150k target)
- Build capital and skills
- Save 30-40% of after-tax income
- Goal: Accumulate $100k-$150k in 2-3 years

**Phase 2 (Ages 37-40): Side Hustle**
- Start business while keeping job
- Validate concept with real customers
- De-risk using employment income
- Goal: Generate $30k-$50k/year from business

**Phase 3 (Ages 40+): Full-Time Business (if validated)**
- Transition when business revenue hits 50% of salary
- Use savings as 12-24 month runway
- Scale aggressively
- Goal: $150k-$300k+ annual profit

**Why this works:** You build capital and skills first (reducing risk), validate your business model before going all-in, and maintain family stability throughout.

If the business doesn't work? You still have a strong career and savings to fall back on.

## The Stay vs. Leave Decision

Here's the framework I developed from the data:

### Reasons to Stay in Australia

**Quality of Life:**
- Excellent healthcare system (for now)
- Top-tier education
- Political stability and safety
- Beautiful environment
- Multicultural, accepting society

**Economic:**
- If you can get into top 20-30% income bracket, life is good
- Strong wages relative to many countries
- Robust financial system
- Natural resource wealth provides economic buffer

### Reasons to Consider Leaving

**Affordability:**
- Housing costs in major cities are globally extreme
- Geographic arbitrage opportunity (earn elsewhere, build wealth faster)
- Cost of living high for goods and services

**Demographics:**
- Aging population will strain economy
- Tax burden likely to increase significantly
- Smaller economy = limited career ceiling for ambitious professionals

**Opportunity:**
- Larger markets (US) offer higher salary potential
- Tax-free jurisdictions (UAE, Singapore) accelerate wealth building
- Geographic diversification reduces single-country risk

### The 5-Year Test

Don't make an emotional decision. Use data.

**Give yourself 5 years (to age 40) with clear targets:**
- Net worth: $300k-$500k
- Annual household income: $150k-$250k
- Housing: Clear path to ownership or strategic renting + investing

**If you hit these targets:** Staying in Australia is viable. You've built enough wealth to buffer against future tax increases.

**If you don't:** Seriously evaluate relocation. You're young enough (40) and your kids are young enough (under 12) to adapt.

**After 45:** If you haven't relocated by then, you probably won't. Kids enter high school, moving becomes much harder.

## Where Would You Go?

If you do decide to leave, here are the data-backed alternatives:

**For Career/Income Maximization:**
- **United States** (particularly tech hubs): 30-50% higher salaries in many fields
- **UAE (Dubai)**: Tax-free income, growing business hub
- **Singapore**: Asian gateway, strong economy, family-friendly

**For Affordability:**
- **Regional Australia first**: Adelaide, Hobart, regional Queensland (test before going international)
- **Portugal/Spain**: EU access, lower cost, good lifestyle (if eligible)
- **Southeast Asia**: Malaysia, Thailand (if remote work possible)

**For Demographics:**
- **United States**: Better demographics due to immigration
- **Southeast Asia**: Younger populations, growth potential

## The Wealth-Building Formula

Success in either scenario—staying or leaving—comes down to one equation:

**Wealth = (High Income × High Savings Rate × Time × Smart Location)**

You control all four variables:

1. **Income:** Choose high-value skills or entrepreneurship
2. **Savings Rate:** Live below means, target 30-40% saved
3. **Time:** You're 35, have 25+ years to compound
4. **Location:** Optimize for taxes, cost of living, opportunity

### Immediate Action Steps

**This Week:**
- Calculate your current net worth (assets minus debts)
- Review 3 months of expenses, identify savings rate
- Research salary benchmarks for your field
- List your top 10 valuable skills

**Next 3 Months:**
- If employment path: Apply to 10-20 high-value roles ($100k+ target)
- If business path: Validate 2-3 business ideas with market research
- Start saving 30-40% of income
- Max out employer superannuation match

**Next 12 Months:**
- Be in new role or have business generating first revenue
- Accumulate $30k-$50k in savings
- Build emergency fund (6 months expenses)
- Make preliminary housing decision (buy vs. rent + invest)

## The Uncomfortable Truth

Australia is still one of the best countries in the world. Your quality of life is higher than 95% of the global population.

But being "good" doesn't mean it's optimal for everyone.

The harsh reality:
- If you're not in the top 20-30% income bracket, Sydney is genuinely difficult
- The aging crisis will increase your tax burden over the next 20 years
- Geographic arbitrage (moving to lower-cost, higher-opportunity locations) can dramatically improve outcomes

**The decision isn't binary.** You don't have to rage-quit Australia or commit forever.

Build optionality:
- Develop portable skills (tech, finance, consulting)
- Save aggressively (gives you options)
- Research visa pathways (US, UK, Singapore, etc.)
- Set clear decision triggers (if X, then Y)

## My Recommendation

**For most 35-year-old families in Sydney:**

**Stay for 5 years.** Use that time to:
- Maximize income ($100k-$150k+ individual, $150k-$250k+ household)
- Build wealth ($300k-$500k net worth by 40)
- Develop portable skills
- Research alternatives

**At age 40, reassess based on data, not emotion:**
- Wealthy enough to buffer aging impacts? Stay.
- Struggling despite high effort? Relocate to better opportunity.
- Hit your targets? You've earned the choice.

**If leaving, do it by 42-45.** Your kids will be 10-15, young enough to adapt. After that, the window closes.

## The Bottom Line

Australia's aging population is a real, data-driven challenge. Combined with extreme housing costs, it creates genuine pressure on young families.

But it's a 15-20 year problem, not an immediate crisis.

You have time to build wealth, develop skills, and create optionality. The next 5 years will determine everything.

Start now. Be strategic. Track metrics. Stay flexible.

Whether you stay or go, make it a choice—not a circumstance.
